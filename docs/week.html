The Week in Littleton · Jun 29 – Jul 5, 2026

School Budget Approved, Drought Bites, and King Street Stirs

*A milestone for Shaker Lane, a dry summer warning, and a stretch of King Street poised for its next chapter define a busy week at the midpoint of 2026.*

Compiled by Littleton Signal across 9 sources

The biggest news to land on Littleton this week came from Boston: the Massachusetts School Building Authority formally approved the budget for the Shaker Lane School construction project, clearing a critical state-level hurdle for replacing a building that is now 64 years old. It is the kind of approval that turns years of planning meetings, community forums, and spreadsheet arguments into something real — a project with a sanctioned price tag and a path forward. The Shaker Lane Building Committee has been meeting regularly, and the School Committee convened a special session on June 25th, a sign that the work of moving from approval to action is already underway. For a town that has been weighing what to do about its aging elementary school for some time, this week marked a turning point.

School's Future

The MSBA's budget approval does not mean shovels in the ground tomorrow, but it means the project is real in the eyes of the state — and that matters enormously for financing and momentum. Community forums held earlier this spring gave residents a chance to weigh in on design direction, and the Building Committee has been keeping the public process moving with regular meetings and posted recordings. The next questions will involve timelines, site logistics, and what the town's share of costs will ultimately look like. Watch for the Building Committee and School Committee to set a more detailed public schedule in the weeks ahead.

A 64-year-old school, a Level 3 drought, and a corridor called King Street: Littleton's summer is anything but quiet.

King Street Rising

While the school story dominated the civic conversation, a quieter but significant thread ran through the land-use calendar: King Street is getting a lot of attention from town boards. The Planning Board has a public hearing scheduled for July 9th on a stormwater management permit and site plan for a retail development at 550 King Street — labeled Phase II, with both a north and south component — and the Conservation Commission has its own public hearing on that same address penciled in for July 21st. Separately, 300 King Street, the site of Castle in the Trees Park, will also come before the Conservation Commission that same evening. That is a notable clustering of scrutiny along a single corridor, and residents who care about how that stretch of town develops will want to pay attention to both boards this month. Also on the Planning Board's July 9th agenda is a public hearing for an affordable senior housing proposal at 410 Great Road — a project that, if it advances, would speak directly to a pressing regional need. The Zoning Board of Appeals joins the action in mid-July with hearings on properties at 27 Lake Warren Drive and 359 King Street.

Dry Summer Warning

Away from the meeting rooms, residents are being asked to change their daily habits in response to something harder to vote on: the weather. Littleton is currently under a Level 3 Critical Drought, the state's most serious drought designation before emergency status, and all nonessential outdoor water use is now prohibited. That means no lawn irrigation, no washing cars in the driveway, no hosing down patios — the kind of summer rituals that feel minor individually but add up across thousands of households. The Light & Water Commissioners met on June 24th, and the Conservation Commission and Clean Lakes Committee have both been active; water conditions will almost certainly remain a central concern for all of them as summer deepens.

Land We Love

Even in a dry summer, there is reason to get outside. The Art Lazarus Trail at Long Lake Park Conservation Area has officially opened, honoring a volunteer who gave 33 years of his life to conservation work in Littleton. A ribbon-cutting ceremony marked the occasion, and the trail now stands as both a recreational asset and a tribute to the kind of quiet, sustained civic commitment that rarely makes headlines but shapes a community profoundly. The Conservation Commission, which meets again on July 7th, also has public hearings coming up on Webster Woods Conservation Land — another reminder of how actively Littleton manages and expands the open spaces that define its character.

Civic Housekeeping

The Select Board held both a regular meeting and a full retreat on June 30th — a sign that the board is doing longer-horizon thinking as the second half of the year begins. Residents who attended Annual Town Meeting in the spring are invited to complete a feedback survey, a useful way for the town to improve how it runs its signature participatory democracy event. Early voting is also available for the upcoming state primary, and with the July 4th holiday landing mid-week, the Finance Committee meeting on July 9th will be one of the first chances for boards to reconvene at full speed after the holiday.
