The Week in Littleton · July 6–12, 2026

Sewers Flow, Schools Rise, and King Street Faces Its Future

*A week of ribbon cuttings and public hearings signals a town actively reshaping itself from the ground up.*

Compiled by Littleton Signal across 9 sources

Littleton marked a quiet but meaningful milestone this week when the new Littleton Common Sewer System was declared fully operational after nearly two years of construction — a ribbon cutting that closes one chapter and opens another for the town center. At the same time, the Planning Board convened Thursday evening to hold public hearings on two projects that could define the next decade of growth along King Street and Great Road: a second retail phase at 550 King Street and an affordable senior housing proposal at 410 Great Road. Taken together, the sewer completion and the development hearings tell the same story — Littleton is building, deliberately, and the decisions being made in meeting rooms this summer will be felt for a generation.

Infrastructure Moment

The ribbon cutting on the Littleton Common Sewer System is the kind of civic accomplishment that rarely gets its due. Sewer projects are unglamorous by nature — they happen underground, they take years, and they only make the news when something goes wrong. This one went right. After nearly two years of construction, the system is fully operational, and it brings a basic piece of modern infrastructure to a part of town that has long needed it. It's the sort of foundational investment that makes everything else — housing, commercial development, a healthy town center — more possible. Also on the utility front, the Littleton Electric Light and Water Department continues to make news in two very different registers. On one hand, LELWD was awarded a state grant of $285,000 to upgrade distribution equipment and reduce the impact of power outages — a meaningful win for grid reliability. On the other, the Littleton Water Department has filed a federal lawsuit to recover costs incurred removing PFAS contamination from the water supply, a fight that speaks to a problem the town did not create but is now shouldering the expense of fixing.

The decisions being made in meeting rooms this summer will be felt for a generation.

King Street at a Crossroads

The Planning Board's Thursday night session was dense with consequence. The board opened public hearings on stormwater and site plans for both the second retail phase at 550 King Street and an affordable senior housing development at 410 Great Road — two projects that together represent the range of pressures on Littleton's commercial and residential corridors. The 550 King Street project will also face the Conservation Commission later this month, on July 21st, adding another layer of review before anything can move forward. Also coming up on the 21st: a Conservation Commission hearing on Webster Woods Conservation Land and another on Castle in the Trees Park at 300 King Street. The concentration of open-space and development hearings on a single evening reflects how much is in motion along the King Street corridor right now. Residents who care about how that stretch of town looks and functions a decade from now have good reason to tune in.

A School Worth Replacing

The campaign to replace Shaker Lane Elementary — a 64-year-old building the town has been studying seriously for over a year — continued to gather momentum this week. The Shaker Lane School Building Committee has been meeting regularly, and a community forum earlier this spring invited residents to share ideas about how a new building might reflect Littleton's identity. The project is moving through the deliberate, multi-step process that Massachusetts school construction requires, and while no ground has been broken, the direction is clear. Meanwhile, Littleton's schools earned recognition that gives the broader conversation some welcome context: Littleton Middle School received a Blue Ribbon nomination, and the high school earned state recognition. These are not small honors, and they serve as a reminder that the push to replace aging facilities is not a story of failure — it's a story of a community that takes its schools seriously enough to invest in them.

Water and Drought

Beneath the meeting agendas and ribbon cuttings, a quieter emergency is unfolding. Littleton is currently under a Level 3 Critical Drought designation from MassDEP, and outdoor watering restrictions are in effect. The Water Department has reminded residents that conservation is not optional right now — it is required. The Clean Lakes Committee met in late June, and with the Conservation Commission active on multiple fronts, water — its quality, its quantity, and its governance — is very much on the town's mind this summer.

Housing and Governance

The Select Board is set to meet Monday evening, July 13th, and one of the items on the agenda reflects a growing priority: a joint meeting with the Housing Authority. Affordable housing has been threading through nearly every corner of civic life in Littleton this week — from the Planning Board hearing on the Great Road senior housing proposal, to the Affordable Housing Trust meeting scheduled for July 14th, to the Zoning Board of Appeals hearings coming up on July 16th for properties on King Street and Lake Warren Drive. The Community Preservation Committee meets July 15th, adding yet another body to the week's housing conversation. What was once a discrete policy area now feels like the connective tissue running through almost everything the town is deciding.
